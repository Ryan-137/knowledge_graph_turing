10 things to know about Alan Turing

Passport photograph of Alan Turing probably taken prior to his visit to the USA in 1936. (Archives Centre, King's College, Cambridge, AMT/K/7/9-16)

1. Turing was a

mathematician

Alan Turing demonstrated a talent for mathematics at a young age. He was educated at Sherborne, an English public school in Dorset, where his maths teacher recognised his ability and let him study on his own instead of following the lessons given to his classmates. He was also fascinated by science and the natural world, but he was uninterested in other subjects. For example, his teachers ‘despaired’ of his performance in English and Latin. He liked to work independently and his work was notoriously scruffy and his handwriting very untidy. He was also an inventor and problem-solver, and his childhood letters to his mother from boarding school describe a number of ‘inventions’ he devised in his free time. You can see a letter here from Alan Turing to his mother and father while he was at Hazelhurst School in 1923, which was written with a fountain pen he had invented.

Alan Turing (left) with school friends, Robin and John Wainwright and Hugh Highet, taken at Waterloo Station on the way to the school carriage at Charing Cross Station. (Archives Centre, King's College, Cambridge, AMT/K/7/3)

2. Turing went to Cambridge and

Princeton Universities

Alan Turing went up to King’s College, Cambridge, in autumn 1931, to study mathematics. He had secured a scholarship to the college in December 1930 and graduated from his undergraduate degree in June 1934. On 16 March 1935, he was elected a Fellow of King’s College, which allowed him to remain in Cambridge and continue his mathematical work.

In 1936, Turing travelled to the USA and studied for three years at Princeton University. During this time, he completed his PhD. At the time of his visit, Princeton was home to the Institute for Advanced Studies, where some of the greatest mathematicians of the age were based, including Alonzo Church, John von Neumann, Albert Einstein, and the physicist Wolfgang Pauli. While intellectually stimulated in the USA, Turing missed Cambridge, and he turned down an offer to remain at Princeton after his PhD.

Turing, Alan Mathison; Graduate Alumni Records, AC105-03, Princeton University Archives, Department of Special Collections, Princeton University Library

3. Turing was

an athlete

In addition to his academic work, Alan Turing was a keen sportsman. He played rugby, hockey and golf at school, and when he went to Cambridge, he took up rowing – competing for King’s College in university competitions. He also played hockey at Princeton with Shaun Wylie, another English mathematician who would later join Turing at Bletchley Park. During and after World War Two, Turing took up running – he would often run long distances to and from work, and once from London to his mother’s home in Guildford. He was successful in athletic competitions, and it is possible that, had he not suffered an injury beforehand, he could have qualified for the marathon at the 1948 London Olympics. In later life, he became friends with the children’s novelist Alan Garner, whom he met while out running, and the pair would meet for runs around Manchester.

Alan Turing placed second in the 3 mile race at the National Physical Laboratory, Boxing Day 1946 (Archives Centre, King's College, Cambridge, AMT/K/7/8)

4. Turing developed

the idea of a computer

In 1936, Turing published an academic paper, ‘On Computable Numbers, with an application to the Entscheidungsproblem’ in the proceedings of the London Mathematical Society. The paper was mostly about whether all mathematics was provable by logical reasoning, a question most recently articulated by German mathematician David Hilbert in 1928. In the paper, Turing proposed an imaginary device which could, by following simple instructions and printing or deleting symbols on an infinitely long tape, solve any mathematical problem or theorem. He did so mostly as part of his argument about the decidability problem, but what he had described was actually a theoretical model for what we would now understand as a computer. This ‘Turing Machine’, as it is now known, is often cited as the inspiration for the computers which surround us today.

5. Turing helped to

break Enigma

In September 1939, Turing joined the Government Code and Cypher School (GC&CS) at Bletchley Park. This was the centre of Britain’s codebreaking effort in World War Two.

Turing’s most famous achievement at Bletchley Park was to develop the concepts behind the ‘Bombe’ machines. These were electro-mechanical devices used to help find the daily key settings of the Enigma cipher machines, used by the German armed forces to encode their radio messages. Thanks to the Bombes, of which over 200 were built, Bletchley Park was able to read many thousands of German messages from early 1940 until the end of the war. This gave Allied commanders a decisive advantage on the various battle fronts, at sea and in the air, and helped bring victory over Nazi Germany in 1945.

An Enigma cipher machine

6. Turing worked on

other secret projects during WW2

As well as breaking Enigma, Turing worked on a variety of other codebreaking projects at Bletchley Park. He contributed to the development of the ‘Colossus’ machines used to break the German Lorenz teleprinter cipher and advised on other enemy codes. He also travelled to the USA to assist with the development of a ‘Bombe’ machine by the US Navy. In 1943, he moved to the nearby communications research centre at Hanslope Park where he worked on a device for speech encryption, which would have allowed telephone conversations to be carried on securely even if an enemy tapped the line, although the finished machine, named ‘Delilah’, was never brought into production. During this period, he also continued his mathematical work on probability, publishing papers both in academic journals as well as writing secret research reports for GC&CS.

7. Turing worked on the first computer

at Manchester University

After World War Two in 1945, Turing joined the National Physical Laboratory in Teddington, London, where he helped to design one of the first electronic computers, known as ‘ACE ’. However, in 1948, before ACE was completed, Turing moved to Manchester University, where he joined a team led by his former Bletchley Park colleague Max Newman, and was appointed as ‘Deputy Director of the Royal Society Computing Laboratory’. The Manchester group produced another computer, known as the ‘Mark 1’, which became operational in March 1949. Turing worked on programming the computer and devised the first manual for its operation. He also used the Manchester Mk 1 to conduct tests of his new theories about the application of mathematics in biology, for example, modelling how the markings on an animal, or the arrangements of leaves on a plant stem, can be determined mathematically.

Alan Turing reading on the porch of a house (Archives Centre, King's College, Cambridge, AMT/K/7/36)

8. Turing pioneered the idea

of Artificial Intelligence

In the 1950s, Turing continued to theorise about the potential of computers in the future. In particular, he was interested in whether, in addition to simply doing calculations, computers could be developed which could learn like humans, or ultimately develop the higher functions of consciousness or creativity. He described these ideas in a paper published in the journal Mind in 1950. He also spoke about these subjects in a 1951 radio broadcast on the BBC, where he summed up his speculations with the question ‘Can digital computers think?’. You can view a scanned script of this broadcast here.

As a result of this work, Turing is now considered to be one of the fathers of machine learning and what we now know as artificial intelligence or AI. He also devised what has become known as the ‘Turing Test’, where he proposed a game in which, by asking only written questions and studying written answers, a player had to determine whether the answers were coming from a human or a machine. The question of whether a machine can, or indeed has, passed this test is widely debated today.

9. Turing

was gay

Alan Turing described himself to Joan Clarke at Bletchley Park as ‘having homosexual tendencies’. In the course of his life, he had several close relationships with other men, although not all of these were of a sexual nature. Both at Cambridge University and at Bletchley Park, his sexual orientation was widely understood and accepted by his colleagues.

Alan Turing’s experience after World War Two has become symbolic in the wider campaign for equality for LGBTQ+ people. When his house was burgled in Manchester in 1952, Turing informed the police that he knew who the burglar might be because he was a friend of Arnold Murray, Turing’s partner at the time. When questioned, Turing openly admitted to a sexual relationship with Murray, which was illegal at the time under the Sexual Offences Act, and he and Murray were prosecuted. Turing was sentenced to a year’s probation and to ‘organotherapy’ – hormone treatment intended to suppress his sexual desires.

After 1967, the law started to change, and Turing would not be prosecuted today. His case was used as part of a campaign to overturn the convictions of other gay men who had been prosecuted under the same laws. Turing himself was granted a Royal Pardon posthumously in 2013.

10. Turing died

young

Alan Turing died of cyanide poisoning at his home in Wilmslow in June 1954. The presence in the house of a partially-completed chemical experiment involving cyanide, and a contaminated apple by his bedside, led the coroner to rule that Turing had died by suicide, ‘while the balance of his mind was disturbed’. Many have questioned this verdict, including Sara Turing, Alan’s mother, who believed the death was accidental and the product of an experimental mishap. However, there is no firm evidence to contradict the conclusions reached by the court at the time. Regardless of the cause, Turing’s death at the age of 41 cut short the life of a man widely described as a mathematical and computing genius.
