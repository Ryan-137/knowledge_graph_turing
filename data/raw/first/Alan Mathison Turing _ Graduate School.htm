Alan Mathison Turing

Father of computer science

  • Degree/year: Ph.D., Mathematics 1938

  • Adviser: Alonzo Church *1927

  • Dissertation Title: Systems of logic based on ordinals

Transformational Impact

Alan Mathison Turing, considered by many to be the “father of computer science,” earned his Princeton Ph.D. in mathematics. 

In January 2008, the Princeton Alumni Weekly (PAW) published a list of the top 25 most influential Princeton alumni Link is external. Alan Turing was second on the list, topped only by U.S. President James Madison *1772. A PAW profile Link is external of Turing captured his life from boyhood through his time at Princeton, his “key role in cracking the Nazis' seemingly impenetrable Enigma code," and his death at the age of 41. 

A pioneer in computational science

When Turing came to the Princeton Graduate School in 1936 after studies at King's College, Cambridge, he had already written the seminal paper, "On Computable Numbers." In that paper, Turing described simple abstract computational devices that his Princeton adviser Alonzo Church '24 *27 — the renowned mathematician, logician, and philosopher — later coined as “Turing machines.” 

Church and Turing were among several mathematicians and logicians who did pioneering work on computation at Princeton in the 1930s, laying the foundation for computer science.  (See Andrew Appel's “The Birth of Computer Science at Princeton in the 1930s Link downloads document.”)

On May 16, 1938, the Princeton Mathematics department commented Link is external on Turing’s Princeton doctoral dissertation, “Systems of logic based on ordinals”: “This thesis is an able and unusual contribution indicative not only of technical capability but also of considerable insight into the problems treated and appreciation of their relationship to other questions…There are also incidental but noteworthy contributions to other branches of mathematics, in particular, to analysis.” 

In May 2012, Princeton hosted a conference Link is external in honor of the centennial of Alan Turing's birth. Featured speakers included Eric Schmidt, a 1976 Princeton alumnus who was Google executive chairman at the time, and eight winners of the Turing Award, often considered the computer science equivalent of the Nobel Prize. 

That same month, Andrew Appel, a 1981 Princeton alumnus and then-chair of the computer science department, published a new edition of Turing's Princeton Ph.D. thesis. In this 2012 video, Appel discusses Turing's legacy.


An enduring icon

Despite his wartime efforts for his native Britain, Turing, an openly gay man, was convicted in 1952 Link is external for violation of England’s laws that criminalized homosexuality. To avoid prison, he had to agree to undergo a series of estrogen injections. He died two years later from what was believed to be suicide. In 2013, the British government granted Turing a posthumous pardon, following an apology for “the appalling way he was treated,” and now upholds an amnesty law informally known as the Alan Turing law.

In September 2019, Princeton unveiled a portrait Link is external of Turing that was added to the Princeton University Art Museum's permanent collection. Jennifer Rexford, professor of computer science and the Gordon Y.S. Wu Professor of Engineering, helped introduce the portrait. Rexford '91 was chair of the computer science department at the time and became provost of Princeton in 2023.

“In the computer science department at Princeton, we owe a tremendous intellectual debt to Alan Turing for our historical strength in theoretical computer science, of course, but also research in artificial intelligence and machine learning, and even working at the intersection of computing with biology, a topic Turing pursued in the last few years of his short life,” Rexford said. “And finally, in the applications of computing to the service of nations and of humanity.”

“For those of us in the LGBTQ community, Turing is an enduring icon, and the growing appreciation of his life and his great contributions to the world are a cause for hope and celebration,” she added.

—This profile was posted in October 2025.
