Alan Turing in Manchester

Alan Turing in Manchester

Published: 07 April 2025

By the time he arrived in Manchester aged 36, the brilliant mathematician Alan Turing (1912–1954) had already achieved incredible things. His codebreaking efforts helped win the Second World War and he was one of the first to propose a machine like a modern computer.

Yet it was in Manchester that Turing first encountered a real universal computer of the type he had imagined, and his life here left a significant legacy. Read on to discover more about Turing's pioneering work on computing in Manchester and the long campaign to pardon him for an historic injustice.

Turing before Manchester

Born in London and educated in mathematics at the universities of Cambridge and Princeton, Alan Turing was only 24 years old when he published his idea for a 'universal computing machine'. In his 1936 mathematics paper 'On Computable Numbers', Turing proposed how a computing machine controlled by symbols on a strip of paper could work on a mathematical problem. Going further, he outlined his idea for a universal computing machine that could do any mathematical problem—a revolutionary idea now seen as the basis for the modern computer.

It is possible to invent a single machine which can be used to compute any computable sequence.

Alan Turing, On Computable Numbers (1936)

During the Second World War, Turing joined the secret Bletchley Park codebreaking centre. His eccentricities at Bletchley became legendary—he cycled in a gas mask to prevent hay fever and chained his mug to a radiator to stop it being stolen—but sometimes people who think differently make all the difference in the world. 

Bletchley's secret work involved many thousands of people, but Turing's mathematical codebreaking methods were vital to their success and he was an inspiration to his colleagues, who knew him as 'Prof'. In the 1930s, Turing’s computing machines had just been an idea, but Bletchley gave him the chance to develop real ones.

Nazi Germany used Enigma machines to automatically turn messages into a complex code before they sent them by radio. With luck, analysis, educated guesswork, and thousands of manual checks, the code could be broken to let the British read secret German radio messages. However, the Enigma machine settings changed daily, meaning that the codebreakers had to work out the new Enigma settings every single day. It was impossible to keep up, but could a machine help?

Building on work started by Polish codebreakers before their country was invaded, Turing and his colleague Gordon Welchman developed a machine to help the codebreakers: the Bombe. These electro-mechanical computers helped work out the Engima settings, allowing Bletchley to read German radio messages quickly enough for the Allies to act on the information. Bletchley's secret work tilted the odds in the Allies' favour and saved thousands of lives. Yet Bletchley was so secret that most of its machines were broken up after 1945 and the codebreakers sworn to secrecy before they returned to their peacetime lives. 

Bletchley's computing machines could only crack codes, but after the war, Turing finally designed a truly universal computer that could have been reprogrammed for any purpose. In 1946, Turing designed the Automatic Computing Engine (ACE), a remarkably sophisticated design for a general-purpose stored program computer. However, the National Physical Laboratory (NPL) where Turing worked failed to fully support the project and it wasn't until 1950 that they built a smaller 'Pilot ACE'. 
 

Frustrated at NPL, Turing took a sabbatical, when an old friend from Bletchley named Max Newman asked if he might be interested in a job as Deputy Director of a computing laboratory Newman was trying to establish at the University of Manchester…

Manchester computers

Turing moved to Manchester in October 1948, where the world's first stored program computer had recently been completed, now known as the 'Manchester Baby'. Built by electronic engineers Frederick Williams, Tom Kilburn and Geoff Tootill, Baby first ran in June 1948, using test programs written by Kilburn and Tootill. 

The prototype Baby computer was far simpler than ACE, but it was the first truly universal computer and gave Turing his first chance to run a proper computer program, for long division. After Geoff Tootill had fixed a bug in Turing's program, it worked perfectly well, making Turing the first person to use Baby apart from its builders. Baby was quickly upgraded into a more powerful computer, the Manchester Mark 1. This design was used by the Ferranti company to make the Ferranti Mark 1, the world's first commercially available stored program computer.

Turing made important contributions to Baby's growth into the Mark 1, but he saw computing differently to the engineering team. He was more concerned with mathematics, theory and programming computers. The engineers were focussed on designing and building the things. There were some tensions, but the most inventive teams are often made of people with different strengths and perspectives, and it was a successful partnership.

Electronic computers are intended to carry out any definite rule of thumb process which could have been done by a human operator working in a disciplined but unintelligent manner. The electronic computer should however obtain its results very much more quickly.

Alan Turing, Programmers' Handbook (1950)

Much of Turing's contribution to the Mark 1 was developing programming facilities. There were no computer programming languages or operating systems at this point, so these were fundamental questions of deciding what operations the machine should do and finding ways to make them happen. 

In 1950 he also wrote one of the first computer manuals, the Programmers' Handbook for the Mark 1. Although the engineers did most of the hardware development, Turing helped develop a random number generator and introduced programming using paper tape machines.

Paper tape programming

Although much of his work in Manchester focussed on software, Turing made a few important contributions to the hardware of the computers.

Programming the prototype Baby computer was a long and tricky business. Users had to type in the whole program one binary bit at time using switches. Then the program disappeared when the computer was switched off. Turing already knew of a better way of feeding information into a machine.

In the 1930s, Turing had imagined a computing machine controlled by symbols on paper tape. At Bletchley Park, messages were converted into teleprinter code punched as holes on paper tape, which could be fed into machines for decoding. Turing introduced the Manchester team to programming with teleprinter tape; he even sourced a paper tape reader for the Manchester Mark 1, probably borrowed from Bletchley.

Users prepared programs on a paper tape by punching holes that represented instructions or data. The program was loaded by feeding the tape into a reader connected to the computer. The tapes could be saved to run the programs again, and the computer was also connected to a machine that could punch program results onto another paper tape. Similar equipment was used not only on the Mark 1, but many later computers too.

Electronic brains

I propose to consider the question, "Can machines think?"

Alan Turing, Computing Machinery and Intelligence (1950)

In 1951 Ferranti delivered the first production model Ferranti Mark 1 Computer to the University of Manchester. Turing's role as Deputy Director of the Computer Laboratory was supposed to involve helping people use this incredible new machine for their computation problems. In practice, the day-to-day task of helping beginners was not the best use of his talents and much of his time was spent considering much bigger questions about the nature of computers.

Computers were a fantastic and little understood new technology in the 1950s. They were often called 'electronic brains' and there was much discussion over their abilities. Turing was fascinated by questions of computer intelligence and whether they could think. He debated machines and the human mind with philosophy professors, spoke on BBC radio about whether calculating machines could think, and caused a stir when he humorously told The Times that it would take a machine to fully appreciate a sonnet written by another machine. In 'Computing Machinery and Intelligence', a foundational 1950 article on artificial intelligence, he proposed an imitation game to see if a computer could fool a person into thinking it was human—now famous as the 'Turing Test'.

Turing also explored the reality of what the Manchester computer could actually do. Alongside mathematics, like investigating prime numbers, he was interested in computer games. The Ferranti Mark 1 wasn't powerful enough to run Turing's own chess program, but he influenced more successful efforts by Ferranti's Dietrich Prinz, who wrote a limited chess problem program, and Christopher Strachey, who got the Mark 1 to play draughts and write love letters. 

Turing's major research interest became computing biological problems. He proposed how patterns that arise in nature, like spots and stripes, had a chemical basis that could be understood mathematically. He developed a deep interest in the Fibonacci sequence, a repeatable sequence of numbers seen throughout nature, such as the numbers of petals on daisies or the spiral patterns on sunflower heads. With the University of Manchester charging commercial customers the huge sum of £20 an hour to use the computer, it caused some amusement that Turing was using his precious computer time to research flowers. Sadly, it was research he never finished.

The Ferranti Mark 1 Computer

Much of Turing's computing at Manchester was done on a Ferranti Mark 1, which was the commercial version of the Manchester Mark 1 Computer, which Turing also helped to develop. Ferranti took the basic design of Manchester University's experimental computer and engineered it into a product for sale. Messy racks of components were replaced by neatly designed cabinets and logic doors. Occupying a whole room, it was used by an operator seated at a control desk fitted with monitors, switches and paper tape machines. About half of Ferranti's programmers in the 1950s were women. Ferranti sold two Mark 1s and seven improved Mark 1* computers. Customers included Stockport aircraft designer Avro and the atomic research centre at Aldermaston.  

Built of over 3,500 electronic valves and 12 cathode ray tubes (used as memory and display screens) linked by six miles of wire, the Ferranti Mark 1 had just a fraction of the power of a modern computer—the main memory was just over 1 kilobyte. However, for the day it was a remarkable machine. The Manchester Evening News told readers that, 'it can work out in a week something that would take men 10 years to solve'. The Museum's collections include several artefacts from Mark 1 and Mark 1* computers, including logic doors, design plans, photos and brochures.

Overturning an injustice

In 1952, Turing, like thousands of others, was criminally convicted under the anti-gay laws of the time for having a relationship with a man. He was sentenced to probation with a course of hormone treatment—chemical castration. 

For Turing, the incident was personally difficult, the hormones had unpleasant side effects, he now had a criminal record, and it ended his clearance to work on top secret projects. He continued to work and his friends supported him, but two years later he was found dead at home in Wilmslow, an apparent suicide by a cyanide laced apple, though many questions remain.

We can only see a short distance ahead, but we can see plenty there that needs to be done.

Alan Turing, Computing Machinery and Intelligence (1950)

Aged just 41, Turing died just as the computer age was beginning. In the decades that followed his reputation has only increased, as computing has changed the world. In 1968 the Association of Computing Machinery created the Turing Award in his name, regarded as the Nobel prize of computing. From the 1970s Bletchley Park's wartime work was declassified and Turing's vital role uncovered. Books, plays, postage stamps and television programmes celebrated Turing's life. 

As his story emerged, Turing became a national hero and an inspiration to the LGBT+ community. Manchester has named buildings and a major road after him. His statue sits on a park bench near Canal Street. And of course, Turing's universal computing machines are everywhere—you're reading this on one now. Yet the historic injustice of how he was treated remained.

As awareness grew about Turing's amazing achievements and terrible treatment, people started to demand something be done. In 2009, Prime Minister Gordon Brown issued an apology on behalf of the British government, but it hardly seemed enough. Scientists, celebrities, politicians and thousands of other people began to support a campaign for a Royal Pardon, writing letters, singing petitions and marching with placards at Pride parades. 

In 2013 their efforts succeeded and a Royal Pardon was issued to Turing after the passing of a Parliamentary Bill, appropriately co-sponsored by a Manchester MP, John Leech. Four years later the government passed 'Turing's Law' to extend the pardon to thousands of other men convicted under similar anti-gay laws.

For a person whose work was so secret and so complicated, Turing has become a perhaps unlikely public figure, but his contributions to history and science were incredible. Cementing Turing's status as an iconic British figure, the Bank of England announced him as the face of the new £50 note in 2019, in a ceremony held right here at the Science and Industry Museum. Yet his true legacy is the modern digital world we live in today.
